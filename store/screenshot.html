Scaling Laws for Local Inference on Unified-Memory Hardware

by A. Researcher · 18 min read

As consumer hardware adopts large unified memory pools, the economics of running capable language models locally shift dramatically. This work measures throughput, latency, and energy across a range of model sizes on commodity unified-memory accelerators.

We find that the memory-bandwidth wall, rather than raw compute, dominates the achievable tokens-per-second for batch-one decoding. Quantization to 4-bit recovers much of the gap, but introduces quality regressions that are workload-dependent.

Across our sweep, models in the 20–30B active-parameter range hit the most favorable point on the quality-versus-interactive-latency curve for single-user reading and research assistants, which informs how a personal inference cluster should be provisioned.

We conclude with practical provisioning guidance and an open benchmark harness for reproducing these measurements on your own hardware.
